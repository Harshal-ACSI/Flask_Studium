What are Atoms?

To learn what atoms do, we need to learn what they exactly are. Atoms are the building blocks of matter. More specifically, they are the smallest constituent unit of matter that possess the properties of the chemical element.[1]

Okay. We now know the meaning of an atom, but what exactly is it? An atom is made of three parts (called subatomic particles):
- Protons
- Neutrons, &
- Electrons
Let's dive deeper into the particles in an atom.

The Protons

The proton is a positively charged particle in an atom. Hence, we say that it has a charge of +1e. The word "proton" came from the Greek word, "prōtos" which means "first" and was given to the hydrogen nucleus by Ernest Rutherford in 1920 (a hydrogen nucleus only has one proton). A proton is also said to have the mass of 1.672 621 925 95 x 10-27 kg.[2]

The Neutrons

Neutrons, unlike electrons and protons, have no electric charge at all (so, it has a charge of 0e). Neutrons are also very slightly larger and heavier than protons. Neutrons and protons, commonly called nucleons, are bound together in the dense inner core of an atom, the nucleus, where they account for 99.9 percent of the atom's mass. [3]

The Electrons

Electrons have a negative electric charge (charge of -1e). But what makes them special is that they are not bound to the nucleus of the atom and have a ridiculously small mass of 9.10938356 x 10-31 (Approximately 1/1837 times the mass of a proton), causing to whizz around the nucleus. This also allows an atom to lose and gain electrons. More on that later...
For now, remember an important rule:
In a stable atom, the number of protons is equal to the number of electrons.This makes the atom's overall charge neutral (0e).

Models of Atoms

There are many models of atoms that are used for different purposes these models can help us visualize atoms much easier. Here are a few models that have been proposed over the years:

Solid Sphere Model John Dalton (1803)
John Dalton had the greek idea that atoms are indivisible ('Atom' came from the Greek word 'Atomos' which means indivisible). However, his theory is wrong as there are subatomic particles, and therefore this model is not used for examining parts of an atom.
Plum Pudding Model J.J.Thomson (1904)
J.J.Thomson discovered electrons inside atoms (for which he won the Nobel Prize) and produced the Plum Pudding Model where the electrons are scattered in a cloud of positive charge. However, this model is rarely used today as it states that an atom does not contain a nucleus.
Nuclear Model Ernest Rutherford (1911)
Ernest Rutherford discovered that atoms have nuclei by firing positively charged alpha particles into an atom. He found out that the atom is mostly empty space and that the positive charge is concentrated in the center of the atom.
Planetary Model Neils Bohr (1913)
Neils Bohr modified the nuclear model by stating that electrons move around the nucleus in fixed orbits. It is clear and very easy to understand. Hence, this is a widely used model used to explain concepts such as valency. We will be using this model later.
Quantum Model Erwin Schrödinger (1926)
Erwin Schrödinger stated that electrons do follow fixed orbits and claimed that it was impossible to find the exact location of an electron. Instead, we have orbitals, which are areas of an atom where electrons are more likely to be. This is the currently most accurate model of an atom.

Atomic Number & Mass

Atoms of different elements can be separated using their atomic number and mass. The atomic number of an atom is the number of protons (or electrons) in an atom. The atomic mass is the total number of protons and neutrons in an atom.

Summary

Atoms are the smallest constituent unit of matter that possesses the properties of the chemical element and is made up of three subatomic molecules:
- Protons [Positively Charged]
- Neutrons [Neutrally Charged]
- Electrons [Negatively Charged]
There is always the same number of electrons as protons in a stable atom. An Atom's atomic number is the number of protons in the atom, and the atomic mass is the total number of protons and neutrons in an atom.
